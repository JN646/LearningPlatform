DSP (Digital Signal Processing)

Digital Signal Processors (DSP) take real-world signals like voice, audio, video, temperature, pressure, or position that have been digitised and then manipulate them. A DSP is designed for performing mathematical functions like "add", "subtract", "multiply" and "divide" very quickly.

Signals need to be processed so that the information can be displayed, analysed, or converted to another type of signal. In the real-world, analogue products detect signals such as sound, light, temperature or pressure and manipulate them. Converters such as an Analog-to-Digital converter then take the real-world signal and turn it into 1's and 0's so computer chips can understand them. The DSP then takes over by capturing the digital information and processing it. It then feeds the digitised information back for use in the real world. This is done either digitally or by using a Digital-to-Analog converter.

A DSP's information can be used by a computer to control such things as security, telephone, home theatre systems, and video compression. Signals may be compressed so that they can be transmitted quickly and more efficiently from one place to another. Signals may also be enhanced or manipulated to improve their quality or provide information that is not sensed by humans (e.g. echo cancellation for mobile phones or computer-enhanced medical images). Although real-world signals can be processed in their analogue form, processing signals digitally provides the advantages of high speed and accuracy.

Because it's programmable, a DSP can be used in a wide variety of applications.
