Audio Development Timeline

1877

Thomas Edison invented the first machine that could record sound using paraffin coated paper.

1886

Alexander Graham Bell invented the graphophone that used wax cylinders to record on.

1888

Edison invented the phonograph, which made use of an electric motor.

1889

Berliner and Suess invented the gramophone which used a large horn for amplification and flat discs for recording.

1898

Valdemar Poulsen patented the first magnetic recording system â€“ a forerunner of tape recording.

1901

Mass production of records now possible.

1904

Production of Shellac discs in Europe.

1927

First 12-inch 40-minute long-playing available. The famous Al Jolson film The Jazz Singer was released, with it synchronised sound-to-picture.

1931-45

Stereo Discs were invented with a different channel of sound inscribed on either side of the V-shaped cut.

1936

Jukeboxes were widespread in cafes and bars across the southern United States.

1941

Large 16-inch records called V-discs were sent to the troops in WW2, containing a mixture of music.

1948

Professional tape recorder manufactured by Ampex. Vinyl 33 1/3 LPs introduced by Columbia. Bell labs invented the transistor, enabling much smaller portable systems to be made.

1953

Pre-recorded stereo tapes available for use in the home.

1957

First Stereo discs released.

1960

Four-track tape cartridge (two separate stereo tracks) available for cars, featuring a continuous looping system.

1963

First compact cassettes available, although these did not become popular for another five years.

1966

Eight track tape systems became a standard option in luxury cars. One-inch eight track recorder introduced by 3M.

1966-67

George Martin produced The Beatles Sergeant Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band a landmark in the use of multitrack recording, which combines two four-track tape recorders so that the tracks could be bounced down and overdubbed.
